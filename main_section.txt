                <h2><strong>Quantum Mechanics</strong>: The Science of the Infinitely Small</h2>



                <!--Exercitiul 10  - Citate b) -->
                <q>If you are not completely confused by quantum mechanics, you do not understand it.</q> — John Wheeler

                <p> <!--Exercitiul 10 - Termeni Stiintifici c) -->
                    <b>Quantum mechanics</b> is a fundamental branch of physics that describes the behavior of <i>matter and energy</i> at the smallest scales—atoms and subatomic particles. 
                    Unlike classical physics, which governs the macroscopic world, quantum mechanics introduces counterintuitive concepts such as <b>superposition</b>, <b>wave-particle duality</b>, and <b>quantum entanglement</b>. 
                    At the core of quantum mechanics lies the <i>wave function</i>, a mathematical representation that describes the probabilities of a particle's state. 
                    The famous <i>Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle</i> states that we cannot simultaneously determine a particle's exact position and momentum with absolute precision. 
                    This probabilistic nature challenges our classical understanding of determinism.
                </p>

                <summary>Details about Quantum Mechanics</summary>
                <p>Quantum mechanics explains the behavior of particles at subatomic scales, where classical physics no longer applies. Key principles include:</p>
                <ul>
                    <li><strong>Wave-Particle Duality:</strong> Particles, like photons and electrons, exhibit both particle-like and wave-like properties.</li>
                    <li><strong>Superposition:</strong> Particles can exist in multiple states at once until measured.</li>
                    <li><strong>Entanglement:</strong> Particles can be entangled, meaning their states are dependent on each other, even when separated by large distances.</li>
                </ul>
            </details>
        
            <details>
                <summary>Quantum Mechanics: Frequently Asked Questions</summary>
                <p><strong>Q: What is quantum entanglement?</strong></p>
                <p>A: Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more particles become interconnected in such a way that the state of one particle directly influences the state of another, no matter the distance between them.</p>
                
                <p><strong>Q: How does quantum computing differ from classical computing?</strong></p>
                <p>A: Quantum computing uses quantum bits (qubits), which can exist in multiple states at once (superposition), while classical computing uses bits that are either 0 or 1.</p>
            </details>
